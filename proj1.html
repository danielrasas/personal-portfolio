Objective

The primary goal was to design a vehicle powered and timed exclusively by chemical reactions to carry a specified variable load across a specific distance. Our team focused heavily on the precision of the stopping mechanism, identifying it as the critical failure point in previous designs. The competition required the vehicle to stop exactly at a distance announced only one hour prior to the run.

The Chemical Clock Mechanism

We utilized an Iodine Clock Reaction (Landolt reaction). By manipulating the initial concentrations of reactants based on a rate law we empirically derived, we could control the time to color change. This color change was detected by a photoresistor circuit which triggered a relay to cut power to the motor.

"The calibration process involved over 50 test runs to minimize the error margin caused by temperature fluctuations, achieving a consistency of ±2 seconds accuracy over a 2-minute run time."

Engineering Challenges

One significant challenge was the ambient temperature sensitivity of the reaction kinetics. To mitigate this, we developed a dynamic lookup table in MATLAB that adjusted the required reactant volumes based on the ambient temperature measured on race day. This 'Cold Start' protocol ensured that our calibration curves remained valid even if the competition venue was significantly warmer or cooler than our lab environment.
